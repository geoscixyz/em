Fresnel Equations

Purpose

Here, we present mathematical expressions which relate the geometry and amplitudes of EM waves at interfaces. This is accomplished by separating the incident wave into two modes: the TE mode and the TM mode. A physical description of each mode is presented along with subsequent derivation. The resulting Fresnel equations allow us to interrelate the amplitudes of the \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{H}\) across the interface.

Setup

Here, we will consider the reflection and transmission of a uniform, linearly polarized, homogeneous plane wave at a horizontal interface (Fig. 59). The incident wave is confined to the xz-plane. The interface is denoted by \(S\), has a normal vector \(\mathbf{\hat n}\) and separates two homogeneous media with physical properties \(\sigma_1\), \(\mu _1\), \(\epsilon_1\) and \(\sigma_2\), \(\mu _2\), \(\epsilon_2\).

For the setup in Fig. 59, the incident wave (\(k_i\)) arrives at angle \(\theta_i\). Once this wave reaches the interface, it breaks into two parts, a reflected wave (\(k_r\)) and a transmitted wave (\(k_t\)). The reflected and transmitted waves travel in directions characterized by angles \(\theta_r\) and \(\theta_t\), respectively.

Electromagnetic waves follow the superposition principle. In order to simplify the math associate with our problem and derive the Fresnel equation, we split the incoming EM wave into two modes. The TE mode represents the portion of the wave associated with electric field components parallel to the surface (Fig. 60). The TM mode represents the portion of the wave associated with magnetic field components coming parallel to the surface (Fig. 61).

Note

The modes are often called the “transverse electric” (denoted by TE or TE z) mode and the tranverse magnetic (denoted by TM or TM z) mode because either electric or magnetic field is transverse to \(z\)-direction.

Fresnel Equation for TE Mode

The geometry, as it relates to the TE mode, is shown in Fig. 60. For the TE mode, the reflection and transmission coefficients are given by:

\[r_{TE} = \frac{\mathbf{E}_r}{\mathbf{E}_i} = \frac{\mu_2 u_1 - \mu_1 u_2} {\mu_2 u_1 + \mu_1 u_2}\]
\[t_{TE} = \frac{\mathbf{E}_t}{\mathbf{E}_i} = \frac{2\mu_2 u_1} {\mu_2 u_1 + \mu_1 u_2}\]

where

\[u_1 = k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i\]

and

\[u_2 = (k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}\]

Derivation

In order to relate the amplitude of the reflected and transmitted waves to that of the incident wave, we required reflection and transmission coefficients for the TE mode, where:

\[r_{TE} = \frac{\mathbf{E}_r}{\mathbf{E}_i}\]
\[t_{TE} = \frac{\mathbf{E}_t}{\mathbf{E}_i}\]

According to the geometry illustrated in Fig. 60, there are no components of \(\mathbf{E}\) along \(\mathbf{k}\) and no components of \(\mathbf{E}\) along \(\mathbf{\hat n}\). Thus:

(143)\[\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{E_i} = \mathbf{k_i} \cdot \mathbf{E_i} = 0\]

and

(144)\[\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{E_t} = \mathbf{\hat n} \cdot \mathbf{E_r} = 0\]

According to the interface conditions stated in constitutive relationships, components of the electric field parallel to surface \(S\) are continuous across the interface. Thus:

(145)\[\mathbf{E}_i + \mathbf{E}_r = \mathbf{E}_t\]

By applying Snell’s law to Eqs. (143) and (144), we obtain:

(146)\[\text{cos} \theta_i \mathbf{E}_i - \text{cos} \theta_r \mathbf{E}_r = \frac{\mu_1 k_2}{\mu_2 k_1} \text{cos} \theta_t \mathbf{E}_t\]

Rearranging Eqs. (145) and (146), we obtain:

(147)\[\mathbf{E}_r = \frac{\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i - \mu_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}} {\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i + \mu_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}} \mathbf{E}_i\]

and

(148)\[\mathbf{E}_t = \frac{2\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i} {\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i + \mu_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}} \mathbf{E}_t\]

where

\[\text{cos}^2 \theta_t = 1 - \text{sin}^2 \theta_t = 1-\Big(\frac{k_1}{k_2}\Big) \text{sin}^2 \theta_i\]

The reflection and transmission coefficients for the TE mode can be written as:

(149)\[r_{TE} = \frac{\mathbf{E}_r}{\mathbf{E}_i} = \frac{\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i - \mu_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}} {\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i + \mu_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}}\]
(150)\[t_{TE} = \frac{\mathbf{E}_t}{\mathbf{E}_i} = \frac{2\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i} {\mu_2 k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i + \mu_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}}\]

Substituting the following:

(151)\[u_1 = k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i\]
(152)\[u_2 = (k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}\]

\(r_{TE}\) and \(t_{TE}\) can ultimately be written as:

(153)\[r_{TE} = \frac{\mu_2 u_1 - \mu_1 u_2} {\mu_2 u_1 + \mu_1 u_2}\]
(154)\[t_{TE} = \frac{2\mu_2 u_1} {\mu_2 u_1 + \mu_1 u_2}\]

Note

In the final form of \(r_{TE}\) and \(t_{TE}\) shown in Eqs (153) and (154), angular information is merged into \(u_1\) and \(u_2\), which are scalar wavenumbers in \(z\)-direction. Namely, horizontal component of the wavenumber do not make any impact to determine \(r_{TE}\) and \(t_{TE}\), and this is driven by transverse electic field to \(z\)-direction.

Fresnel Equation for TM Mode

The geometry, as it relates to the TM mode, is shown in Fig. 61. For the TM mode, the reflection and transmission coefficients are given by:

\[r_{TM} = \frac{\mu_2 u_2 k_1^2 - \mu_1 u_1 k_2^2} {\mu_2 u_2 k_1^2 + \mu_1 u_1 k_2^2}\]
\[t_{TM} = \frac{2\mu_1 u_1 k_2^2} {\mu_2 u_2 k_1^2 + \mu_1 u_1 k_2^2}\]

where

\[u_1 = k_1 \text{cos} \theta_i\]

and

\[u_2 = (k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2}\]

Derivation

In order to relate the amplitude of the reflected and transmitted waves to that of the incident wave, we required reflection and transmission coefficients for the TM mode, where:

(155)\[r_{TM} = \frac{\mathbf{H_r}}{\mathbf{H_i}} = \frac{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_r}{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_i}\]
(156)\[t_{TM} = \frac{\mathbf{H_t}}{\mathbf{H_i}} = \frac{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_t}{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_i}\]

According to the geometry illustrated in Fig. 61, there are no components of \(\mathbf{H}\) along \(\mathbf{k}\) and no components of \(\mathbf{H}\) along \(\mathbf{\hat n}\). Thus:

(157)\[\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{H}_i = \mathbf{k}_i \cdot \mathbf{H}_i = 0\]

and

(158)\[\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{H}_t = \hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{H}_r = 0\]

According to the interface conditions stated in constitutive relationships, components of the magnetic field parallel to surface \(S\) are continuous across the interface. Thus:

(159)\[\mathbf{H}_i + \mathbf{H}_r = \mathbf{H}_t\]

And by applying Snell’s law to Eqs. (157) and (158), we obtain:

(160)\[\text{cos} \theta_i \mathbf{H}_i - \text{cos} \theta_r \mathbf{H}_r = \frac{\mu_1 k_2}{\mu_2 k_1} \text{cos} \theta_t \mathbf{H}_t\]

Rearranging Eqs. (159) and (160), we obtain:

(161)\[\mathbf{H}_r = -\frac{\mu_2 k_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2} - \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i} {\mu_2 k_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2} + \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i} \mathbf{H}_i\]
(162)\[\mathbf{H}_t = \frac{2 \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i} {\mu_2 k_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2} + \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i} \mathbf{H}_i\]

where

\[\text{cos}^2 \theta_t = 1 - \text{sin}^2 \theta_t = 1-\Big(\frac{k_1}{k_2}\Big) \text{sin}^2 \theta_i\]

Thus the reflection and transmission coefficients for TM mode can be written as:

(163)\[r_{TM} = \frac{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_t}{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_i} = - \frac{\mathbf{H}_r}{\mathbf{H}_i} = \frac{\mu_2 k_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2} - \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i}{\mu_2 k_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2} + \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i}\]
(164)\[t_{TM} = \frac{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_t}{\hat{\mathbf{n}}\times \mathbf{E}_i} = \frac{\mathbf{H}_t}{\mathbf{H}_i} = \frac{2 \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i}{\mu_2 k_1(k_2^2-k_1^2 \text{sin}^2 \theta_i)^{1/2} + \mu_1k_2^2 \text{cos} \theta_i}\]

Substituting these with Eqs. (151) and (152) yields:

(165)\[r_{TM} = \frac{\mu_2 u_2 k_1^2 - \mu_1 u_1 k_2^2} {\mu_2 u_2 k_1^2 + \mu_1 u_1 k_2^2}\]
(166)\[t_{TM} = \frac{2\mu_1 u_1 k_2^2} {\mu_2 u_2 k_1^2 + \mu_1 u_1 k_2^2}\]

Question

  • We defined reflection coefficient of TM mode \(r_{TM}\) as ratio between tangential electric field of incidence and reflection as shown in Eq. (155). However, we derived ratio of \(\mathbf{H}_i\) and \(\mathbf{H}_r\) then multipy -1 to obtain \(r_{TM}\), why is that? (Hint: See direction of \(\mathbf{E}\) and \(\mathbf{H}\) in Fig. 61)

Fresnel Equations for Normal Incidence

When incidence is normal (\(\theta_i\) =0), Eqs. (153) and (165) can be reduced to:

\[r_{TE} = r_{TM} = \frac{\mu_2 k_1 - \mu_1 k_2} {\mu_2 k_1 + \mu_1 k_2}\]
